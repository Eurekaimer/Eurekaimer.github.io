    Chapter 3 : Conditional Probability and Independence

    Conditional Probabilities

    [!question]
    Motivation

    Suppose we toss 2 dice, and suppose all the possible outcomes equally occur. Suppose further that we observe that the first die is a 3, what is the probability that the sum of the 2 dice equals 8?

    [!example]
    Example3.1(Slandard discussion)
    $$
    F={the first die is 3}
    \E={the sum is 8 }
    $$
    We want to discuss
    $$
    \begin{align}
    P(E|F)&=\frac{P(E\cap F)}{P(F)}
    \&=\frac{P(1^{th} die is 3 ,2^{th} die is 5)}{P(F)}
    \&=\frac{\frac{1}{36}}{\frac{6}{36}}=\frac{1}{6}
    \end{align}
    $$

    Conditional probability

    [!NOTE]
    Definition 3.1 ([[Conditional probability]])

    Let \((S,\mathcal{A},P)\) be a probability space and \(F\in \mathcal{A}\) be an event such that \(P(F)>0\) ,we define the conditional probability that an event \(E\in\mathcal{A}\) occurs given or knowing that \(F\) has occurred,which is denoted by \(P(E|F)\),as follows:
    $$
    P(E|F)=\frac{P(E\cap F)}{P(F)}
    $$

    [!tip]
    Remark 3.2

    Conditional probability gives us a prediction upon partial information. \(F\) acts as a new but reduced sample space.

    \(P(·|F)\) as a probability measure

    [!NOTE]
    Proposition 3.3
    Let \((S, \mathcal{A}, P)\)be a probability space and \(F \in \mathcal{A}\) be an event such that \(P(F) > 0\). The function mapping \(P_F : \mathcal{A} → R\) defined by \(P_F (E) = P(E|F)\) is a probability measure on \((S, \mathcal{A})\).

    Proof

    We need to verify \(P_F\) satisfies 3 axioms

    1. nonnegativity For amy \(E\in \mathcal{A},P_F(E)\ge0\)
    2. \(P_F(S)=1\)
    3. For \(E_1,...,E_n\) countable,mutually exclusive
    \[ P(\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty}E_i)=\sum_{i=1}^{\infty}P_{F}(E_i) \]

    Proof of 1

    \[ P_F(E)=P(E|F)=\frac{P(EF)}{P(F)}\ge0 $$ `Proof of 2` $$ P_F(S)=P(S|F)=\frac{P(SF)}{P(F)}=1 $$ `Proof of 3` $$ \begin{align} P_F(\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty}E_i)&=P(\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty}E_i|F)=\frac{P((\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty}E_i)\cap F)}{P(F)} \\&=\frac{P(\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty}(E_i\cap F))}{P(F)}=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{\infty}P(E_i\cap F)}{P(F)} \\&=\sum_{i=1}^{\infty}P(E_i|F)=\sum_{i=1}^{\infty}P_F(E_i) \end{align} \]

    [!NOTE]
    Proposition 3.4
    Let \(S\) be a finite sample space and consider the associated probability space \((S, \mathcal{A}, P)\) with uniform probability measure \(P\). Let \(F \in A\) be an event with \(P(F) > 0\), the conditional probability of event \(E \in A\) given that F has occurred can be computed as:
    $$
    P(E|F)=\frac{|E\cap F|}{|F|}
    $$

    [!example]
    Example 3.2
    Suppose we toss 2 dice, and suppose all the possible outcomes equally occur. Suppose further that we observe that the first die is a 3, what is the probability that the sum of the 2 dice is odd?

    Solution
    $$
    \begin{align}
    \E_1&={the sum is odd}
    \P(E_1|F)&=\frac{|E_1\cap F|}{|F|}
    \&=\frac{3}{6}=\frac{1}{2}
    \end{align}
    $$

    [!example]
    Example 3.3 (Coin flipping)
    A coin is flipped twice. Suppose that all four outcomes in sample space \(S = {(h,h),(h,t),(t,h),(t,t)}\) are equally likely, what is the probability that both flips land on heads, given that (a) the first flip lands on heads? (b) at least one flip lands on heads?

    Solution
    $$
    \begin{align}
    E&={(h,h)}
    \(a)F_1&={(h,h)(h,t)}
    \P(E|F_1)&=\frac{|E\cap F_1|}{|F|}=\frac{1}{2}
    \(b)F_2&={(t,t)}
    \P(E|F_2)&=\frac{|E\cap F_2|}{|F|}=\frac{1}{3}
    \end{align}
    $$

    [!NOTE]
    Proposition 3.5 (Multiplication rule)
    Let \(E_1, . . . , E_n\) be a sequence of \(n \in N\), n ≥ 1 events. Then we have
    $$
    P(\bigcap_{i=1}^{n}E_i)=P(E_1)P(E_2|E_1)P(E_3|E_1\cap E_2)...P(E_n|\bigcap_{i=1}^{n-1}E_i)
    $$

    Proof
    $$
    RHS=P(E_1)\frac{P(E_1E_2)}{P(E_1)}\frac{P(E_1E_2E_3)}{P(E_1E_2)}...\frac{P(\bigcap_{i=1}^{n}E_i)}{P(\bigcap_{i=1}^{n-1}E_i)}=P(\bigcap_{i=1}^{n}E_i)
    $$

    [!example]
    Example 3.4 (Poker game revisited)
    A deck of 52 cards is randomly divided into 4 piles of 13 cards each. Compute the probability that each pile has exactly 1 ace.

    Solution

    Let \(E_i\) be the event that pile i has exactly 1 ace(i=1,2,3,4)
    $$
    P(E_1E_2E_3E_4)=P(E_1)P(E_2|E_1)P(E_3|E_1E_2)P(E_4|E_1E_2E_3)
    \=\frac{\binom{4}{1}\binom{48}{12}}{\binom{52}{13}}\frac{\binom{3}{1}\binom{36}{12}}{\binom{39}{13}}\frac{\binom{2}{1}\binom{24}{12}}{\binom{26}{13}}
    $$

    [!example]
    Example 3.5 (The matching problem[[#^matching-problem]] revisited)
    In the matching problem we discussed in the previous class, it was shown that the probability that no matches occur when \(N\) people randomly select from \(N\) mixed-up hats, denoted by \(P_N\) , is given by
    $$
    P_N=\sum_{i=0}^{N}(-1)^i\frac{1}{i!}=1-1+\frac{1}{2!}-\frac{1}{3!}+...
    $$
    What is the probability that exactly k of the N people have matches?

    Solution

    \(P_N:\) the probability that none of N people gets his own hat

    \(P_N\approx e^{-1}\) when N is large

    Firstly, a group of k people can be chosen in \(\binom{N}{k}\) ways.

    For one fixed k-people group without loss of generality ([[WLOG]]),we label them as people 1,2,...,k.

    Let \(E\) be the event that \(1,2,...,k\), have matchs.

    Let \(G\) be the event that no matchs occur among people \(k+1,...,N\),(N-k people)
    $$
    P(G|E)=P_{N-k}=\sum_{i=0}^{N-k}(-1)^i\frac{1}{i!}
    $$
    the probability that exactly k people have matchs is \(\binom{N}{k}P(E\cap G)\)

    \(P(E\cap G)=P(E)P(G|E)=P(E)P_{N-k}\) ,

    Let \(E_i\)={the \(i^{th}\) person gets his own hat}

    By multiplication rule
    $$
    \begin{align}
    P(E)&\overset{directly from permutation}{=}P(\bigcap_{i=1}^{k}E_i)=P(E_1)...P(E_k|\bigcap_{i=1}^{k-1}E_i)
    \&=\frac{1}{N}\frac{1}{N-1}...\frac{1}{N-k+1}
    \&=\frac{(N-k)!}{N!}
    \end{align}
    $$
    The probability that exactly k people get their own hat is
    $$
    \binom{N}{k}\frac{(N-k)!}{N!}P_{N-k}=\frac{1}{k!}P_{N-k}\approx\frac{1}{k!}e^{-1}
    $$
    for a fixed k and N is large

    [[Bayes's Formula]]

    [[First Bayes's formula]]

    [!NOTE]
    Proposition 3.6 (First Bayes’s formula)
    Let \(E, F\) be two events such that \(P(E) > 0, P(F) > 0\). We have that
    $$
    P(F|E)=\frac{P(E|F)P(F)}{P(E)}
    $$

    Proof

    By definition of conditional probability
    $$
    P(F|E)=\frac{P(F\cap E)}{P(E)}=\frac{P(F)P(E|F)}{P(E)}
    $$

    [!example]
    Example 3.6
    According to a study, 0.5% of the population has symptoms of video game addiction. The study highlights that the proportion of addicts is 2% among teenagers. We also know that there are 15% teenagers in the population. What is the probability that a randomly selected addict is a teenager?

    Solution

    Let A be the event that a preson is on addicts
    T be the event that a person is a teenager
    $$
    P(A)=0.005 P(A|T)=0.02 P(T)=0.15
    \P(T|A)=\frac{P(A|T)P(T)}{P(A)}=0.6
    $$

    [[Law of total probability]]

    [!important]
    [[Law of total probability]]
    Let \(E \in A\) be an event and \(\{F_i\}_{i≥1}\) be a countable partition of the sample space \(S\) (that is, \(\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty}F_i = S\ and \ F_i \cap F_j = \emptyset \ for\ i \neq j)\),
    $$
    P(E)=\sum_{j=1}^{\infty}P(E\cap F_i)
    $$
    Now, with the knowledge of conditional probabilities, we can rewrite the expression as follows:
    $$
    P(E)=\sum_{j=1}^{\infty}P(E|F_i)P(F_i)
    $$
    In particular,
    $$
    P(E)=P(E|F)P(F)+P(E|F^c)(1-P(F))
    $$

    [!example]
    Example 3.7
    According to a study, 0.5% of the population has symptoms of video game addiction. The study highlights that the proportion of addicts is 2% among teenagers. We also know that there are 15% teenagers in the population. What is the probability that a non-teenager is an addict?

    Solution

    We want to study \(P(A|T^c)\) Note that
    $$
    \begin{align}

    P(A)&=P(A|T)P(T)+P(A|T^c)(1-P(T))
    \0.05&=0.02·0.15+P(A|T^c)·0.85
    \P(A|T^c)&\approx0.00235
    \end{align}
    $$

    [!example]
    Example 3.8([[Polya's urn scheme]])
    There is an urn containing r red balls and b black balls, suppose each ball can be selected equally likely. Now each time we randomly select one ball, observe the color, then return the ball with s more balls of the same color. Let \(R_i\) denote the event that at the \(i^{th}\) selection, one gets a red ball. Find \(P(R_2), P(R_n)\).

    Solution

    Note that \(R_{1}^c\) denotes that one get a black at the \(1^{th}\) selection
    $$
    \begin{align}
    P(R_{2})&=P(R_2|R_1)P(R_1)+P(R_2|R_1^c)P(R_1^c)
    \&=\frac{r+s}{r+b+s}\frac{r}{r+b}+\frac{r}{r+b+s}\frac{b}{r+b}
    \&=\frac{r}{r+b}
    \end{align}
    $$

    In fact, \(P(R_n)=\frac{r}{r+b}\) for any value of s . We prove by induction. We've shown it's true for n-2.Now we suppose it's true for n.

    Let's check for n+1

    \[ \begin{align} P(R_{n+1})&=P(R_{n+1}|R_1)P(R_1)+P(R_{n+1}|R_1^c)P(R_1^c) \\&=\frac{r+s}{r+b+s}\frac{r}{r+b}+\frac{r}{r+b+s}\frac{b}{r+b} \\&=\frac{r}{r+b} \end{align} \]

    [!tip] Remark
    The most important thing is discussing \(P(R_{n+1}|R_1)\) is the same as thinking "we start with r+s (r) red balls , b (b+s) blue balls ,the probability we get a red ball at the \(n^{th}\) round". (If we start with \(R_1^c\))

    Make the n+1 to be n (According to the suppose n is true).

    So \(P(R_{n+1}|R_1)=\frac{r+s}{r+b+s}\) ,\(P(R_{n+1}|R_1^c)=\frac{r}{r+b+s}\)

    The following example discusses that whether the conditional probability is always greater than the probability or not.

    Compare conditional probability and unconditional probability

    [!example] Example 3.9
    There is certain probability for the hypothesis of guilt for someone. Now some
    new evidence shows up, how does the probability of hypothesis change?

    H=hypothesis guilit, E=evidence shows up, compare \(P(H|E)\) and \(P(H)\)

    Conclusion. If \(P(H|E) ≥ P(H)\), that means the new evidence is in support of
    the hypothesis, that is,
    $$
    P(E|H) \ge P(E|H^c)
    $$
    In other words, any new evidence can be considered to be in support of a
    particular hypothesis only if its occurrence is more likely when the hypothesis
    is true than when it is false.

    ^Eg3-9

    [!tip] Remark
    The discussion of Example 3.9[[#^Eg3-9]] can be expressed compactly in terms of the change in the odds.

    \[ P(H|E)=\frac{P(E|H)P(H)}{P(E|H)P(H)+P(E|H^c)P(H^c)} $$ If $P(H|E)\ge P(H)$,it means the evidence is in support of the hypothesis $$ \begin{align} &\Leftrightarrow P(E|H)\ge P(E|H)P(H)+P(E|H^c)P(H^c) \\&\Leftrightarrow P(E|H)\ge P(E|H^c) \end{align} \]

    [!NOTE]
    Definition 3.7
    The odds of an event A are \(\frac{P(A)}{P(A^c )}=\frac{P(A)}{1 − P(A)}\).
    The odds tell us that how much more likely it is that the event A occurs than it is that it does not occur.

    Back to Example 3.9 discussion, the new odds under the new evidence are

    $$
    \frac{P(H|E)}{P(H^c|E)}=\frac{\frac{P(E|H)P(H)}{P(E)}}{\frac{P(E|H^C)P(H^C)}{P(E)}}=\frac{P(H)}{P(H^c)}\frac{P(E|H)}{P(E|H^c)}
    $$
    That is, the \(\color{blue}{new\ odds\ of\ H}\) are the \(\color{blue}old\ odds\) multiplied by the \(\color{blue}ratio\) of the conditional probability of the new evidence given that H is true to the conditional probability given that H is not true.

    Second Bayes's formula

    [!important]
    [[Second Bayes’s formula]]
    Let \(E \in A\) be an event and \(\{F_i\}^n_{i=1}\) be a countable partition of the sample space \(S\) (that is, \(\bigcup\limits _{i=1}^nFi = S \ and\ F_i ∩ F_j =\emptyset \ for\ i \neq j\)). For a given \(i = 1, . . . , n\), we have that
    $$
    P(F_i|E) = \frac{P(E|F_i)P(F_i)}{\sum\limits_{j=1}^{n}P(E|F_j)P(F_j).}
    $$

    Proof

    Using first Bayes’s formula and law of total probability.

    \(\dfrac{P(H)}{P(H^c)}\)(unconditional probability), \(\dfrac{P(H|E)}{P(H^c|E)}\)(conditional probability)

    Smartphones of some brand are produced in three factories. They are responsible for 20%, 30%, 50% of the output, respectively. The fraction of defective smartphones produced is: for the first factory, 5%; for the second, 3%; for the third, 1%. If a phone is chosen at random from the total output, and turns out to be defective, what is the probability that it was produced by the third factory.

    Example 3.11
    3 cards are identical in form, both sides of the first card are colored red, both sides of the second card are colored black, one side of the third card is colored red and the other black. The 3 cards are mixed up in a hat, and 1 card is randomly selected and put down on the ground. If the upper side is red, what is the probability that the other side is colored black?

    Let \(RR,BB,RB\) be the event that the chosen card is red-red, black-black, red-black card, respectively.

    Let \(R\) be the event that the upper side of a chosen card is red.

    we want the probability \(P(RB|R)\)
    $$
    P(RB|R)=\frac{P(R|RB)P(RB)}{P(R|RR)P(RR)+P(R|RB)P(RB)+P(R|BB)P(BB)}=\frac{\frac{1}{2}\frac{1}{3}}{1\frac{1}{3}+\frac{1}{2}\frac{1}{3}}=\frac{1}{3}
    $$

    Independent Events

    [!NOTE]
    Definition 3.9
    Let \((\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, P)\) be a probability space and \(E, F \in \mathcal{A}\) are two events with \(P(E) > 0, P(F) > 0\). \(E, F\) are said to be independent if one of the following equivalent identities holds
    + \(P(E \cap F) = P(E)P(F)\)
    + \(P(E|F) = P(E)\)
    + \(P(F|E) = P(F)\)

    [!example]
    Example 3.12
    A card is selected at random from an ordinary deck of 52 playing cards. If \(E\) is the event that the selected card is an ace and \(F\) is the event that it is a spade, show that \(E, F\) are independent.

    Solution
    $$
    \begin{align}
    &P(E)=\frac{4}{52}=\frac{1}{13},P(F)=\frac{13}{52}=\frac{1}{4}
    \P(E\cap F)&=P(the selected card is the ace of spades)
    \&=\frac{1}{52}=P(E)P(F)

    \end{align}
    $$
    \(E,F\) are independece

    [!NOTE]
    Proposition 3.10
    If events \(E, F\) are independent, then so are:
    + \(E \ and\ F^c\)
    + \(E^c \ and\ F\)
    + \(E^c \ and\ F^c\)

    Proof

    We only verify \(E\) and \(F^c\) are independence (the other two are lefts as exercise)

    We want to show \(P(E\cap F^c)=P(E)P(F^c)\)
    $$
    \begin{align}
    LHS&=P(E)-P(E\cap F) (law of total probability)
    \&=P(E)-P(E)P(F) (E,F are independence)
    \&=P(E)P(F^c)=RHS
    \end{align}
    $$

    [!example] Example 3.13
    You roll one die followed by another die. Let \(A\) be the event that the sum of the dice is 9, \(B\) be the event that the first die lands on an even number, \(C\) be the event that the second die lands on an odd number. Analyze the relation among \(A, B, C\).

    Solution
    $$
    \begin{align}
    P(A)&=\frac{|(3,6)(4,5)(5,4)(6,3)|}{36}
    \&=\frac{4}{36}=\frac{1}{9}
    \end{align}
    $$
    By symmetry, \(P(B)=P(C)=\frac{1}{2}\)

    \(P(AB)=\frac{2}{36}=\frac{1}{18}=P(A)P(B)\), \(A,B\) are independent

    \(P(AC)=\frac{1}{18}=P(A)P(C)\),\(P(BC)=P(B)P(C)\) , \(A,C\) are independent, \(B,C\) are independent

    Consider \(A,B,C\) are pairwise independent \(P(ABC)=\frac{1}{18}\neq P(A)P(B)P(C)\)

    Independent of n events

    [!NOTE]
    Definition 3.11
    Let \(E_1, . . . , E_n\) be a sequence of events, we say \(\{E_i\}^n_{i=1}\) are pairwise independent if \(E_i\)and \(E_j\) are independent, that is, \(P(E_i\cap E_j) = P(E_i)P(E_j)\) for all \(i \neq j\).

    We say that \(\{E_i\}^n_{i=1}\) are independent if for all subsets \(I ⊂ \{1, 2, . . . , n\}\),
    $$
    P(\bigcap_{i\in I}E_i)=\prod_{i\in I}P(E_i)
    $$
    For instance, for n = 3, if \(E_1, E_2, E_3\) are independent, that means
    \(P(E1 ∩ E2) = P(E1)P(E2), P(E1 ∩ E3) = P(E1)P(E3), P(E2 ∩ E3) = P(E2)P(E3);\)\(P(E1 ∩ E2 ∩ E3) = P(E1)P(E2)P(E3).\)

    [!example]
    Example 3.14: [[The gambler’s ruin problem]]
    Two gamblers, \(A\) and \(B\), bet on the outcomes of successive flips of a coin.
    On each flip, if the coin comes up heads, \(A\) collects 1 unit from \(B\); if it comesup tails, \(A\) pays 1 unit to \(B\).
    They continue to do this until one of them run out of money.
    Assume that the flips are independent and each flip results in a head with probability \(p\), what is the probability that A ends up with all money if he starts with \(i\) units and B starts with \(N − i\) units?

    Solution

    Let \(E_i\) be the event that \(A\) collects all the money if he starts with \(i\) units ,\(B\) starts with \(N-i\) units .Want to study \(P(E_i)=P_i\), \(P(E_N)=1=P_N\)

    Let \(H\) be the event that the first flip lands on heads and \(P(H)=p\).
    $$
    P_i=P(E_i|H)P(H)+P(E_i|H^c)P(H^c)=pP(E_i|H)+(1-p)P(E_i|H^c)
    $$
    Given the first flip landing on \(heads\ \color{red}{tails}\), the probability that \(E_i\) occurs is the SAME as analyzing the probability that \(A\) collects all the money if he starts with \(i+1\ \color{red}{i-1}\) units \(B\) starts with \(N-i-1\ \color{red}{N-i+1}\) units (\(E_{i+1}\ E_{i-1}\))
    $$
    pP(E_i|H)+(1-p)P(E_i|H^c)=pP_{i+1}+(1-p)P_{i-1}
    $$

    \[ \begin{align} P_i&=pP_{i+1}+(1-p)P_{i-1} \\p(P_{i+1}-P_{i})&=(1-p)(P_i-P_{i-1}) \\P_{i+1}-P_i&=\frac{1-p}{p}(P_{i}-P_{i-1}) \end{align} \]

    We let \(q=1-p\) note that \(P_0=0\)
    $$
    P_2-P_1=\frac{q}{p}P_1
    \P_3-P_2=\frac{q}{p}(P_2-P_1)=(\frac{q}{p})^2P_1
    \P_i-P_{i-1}=(\frac{q}{p})^{i-1}P_1
    \P_N-P_{N-1}=(\frac{q}{p})^{N-1}P_1
    $$
    Adding the first $ i-1$ indentities:
    $$
    \begin{align}
    P_i&=[1+\frac{q}{p}+(\frac{q}{p})^2+···+(\frac{q}{p})^{i-1}]P_1
    \&=\left{\begin{matrix}
    iP_1 if p=q=\frac{1}{2}
    \ \frac{1-(\frac{q}{p})^i}{1-\frac{q}{p}}P_1 if p\neq\frac{1}{2}
    \end{matrix}\right.
    \end{align}
    $$
    With \(P_N=1\)
    $$
    P_1=\left{\begin{matrix}
    \frac{1}{N} if p=\frac{1}{2} \
    \frac{1-\frac{q}{p}}{1-(\frac{q}{p})^N} if p\neq\frac{1}{2}
    \end{matrix}\right.
    $$
    then
    $$
    P_i=\left{\begin{matrix}
    \frac{i}{N} if p=\frac{1}{2} \
    \frac{1-(\frac{q}{p})^i}{1-(\frac{q}{p})^N} if p\neq\frac{1}{2}
    \end{matrix}\right.
    $$

    [!example]
    Example 3.15
    A person has two coins. The first coin comes up heads with probability 1/4 and tails with probability 3/4. The second coin comes up heads with probability 3/4 and tails with probability 1/4. One coin is selected and then flipped twice. Each coin can be equally likely selected.
    + Let \(H_1\) be the event that the first flip is heads and let \(H_2\) be the event that
    the second flip is heads. Show that \(H_1, H_2\) are not independent.
    + Let \(F\) be the event that the first coin is selected. Show that \(P(H_1 ∩ H_2|F) = P(H_1|F)P(H_2|F)\)

    Solution

    \(P(H_1)=P(H_1|F)P(F)+P(H_1|F^c)P(F^c)=\frac{1}{2}\frac{1}{4}+\frac{1}{2}\frac{3}{4}=\frac{1}{2}\)

    Similarly, \(P(H_2)=\frac{1}{2}\) .But note that
    $$
    \begin{align}
    P(H_1H_2)=P(H_1H_2|F)P(F)+P(H_1H_2|F^c)P(F^c)
    \=\frac{1}{2}(\frac{1}{4})^2+\frac{1}{2}(\frac{3}{4})^2=\frac{5}{16}\neq P(H_1)P(H_2)\end{align}
    $$
    $$
    P(H_1\cap H_2|F)=\frac{P(H_1\cap H_2F)}{P(F)}=\frac{\frac{1}{32}}{\frac{1}{2}}=\frac{1}{16}
    \P(H_1|F)P(H_2|F)=\frac{1}{4}\frac{1}{4}=\frac{1}{16}
    $$

    Conditional Probability

    [!NOTE]
    Definition 3.12
    Let \(E, F, G\) be three events with \(P(G) > 0\). We say that \(E\) and \(F\) are conditionally independent given \(G\) (or that conditioned on \(G\) the events \(E\) and \(F\) are independent) if
    $$
    P(E ∩ F|G) = P(E|G)P(F|G)
    $$
    An equivalent definition for \(E, F\) conditionally independent given \(G\) is
    $$
    P(E|FG) = P(E|G)
    $$
    It can be interpreted as: Given that \(G\) occurs, the conditional probability that \(E\) occurs is unchanged by information as to whether or not \(F\) occurs.

    [!tip]
    Remark 3.13
    It is easy to check that if \(E, F\) are conditionally independent given \(G\), then so are \(E\) and \(F^c\). It is also easy to extend this to three or more events being conditionally independent given \(G\).

    [!NOTE]
    Proposition 3.14
    \(\{F_i\}^n_{i=1}\) is a partition of the sample space \(S\), that is, \(\bigcup\limits _{i=1}^{n}F_i = S\) and \(Fi ’s\) are mutually exclusive. Then,
    $$
    P(E|F) =\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n} P(E|FF_i)P(F_i|F)
    $$
    In particular, \(P(E|F) = P(E|FF_1)P(F_1|F) + P(E|FF^c_1)P(F^c_1|F).\)

    Proof

    Direct proof by using definitions (exercise)

    Alternative proof by thinking \(P_F(\cdot)=P(\cdot|F)\) as a probability measure

    By law of total probability
    $$
    \begin{align}
    P(E|F)=P_F(E)&=\sum_{i=1}^nP_F(E|F_i)P_F(F_i)({\color{green}{P(F_i|F)}})
    \
    P_F(E|F_i)&=\frac{P_F(EF_i)}{P_F(F_i)}=\frac{P(EF_i|F)}{P(F_i|F)}
    \&=\cdot \cdot \cdot=P(E|FF_i)
    \end{align}
    $$

    [!example]
    Example 3.16: [[ Laplace’s rule of succession]]
    There are \(k + 1\) coins in a box. When flipped, the ith coin will turn up heads with probability \(\frac{i}{k}\),\(i = 0, 1, . . . , k\). A coin is randomly selected from the box and is then repeatedly flipped. If the first n flips all result in heads, what is the conditional proability that the \((n + 1)\) flip will do likewise?

    Solution

    Let \(F_n\) be the event that the first \(n\) flips result in heads.Let \(H\) be the event that the first \(n+1\) flips result in heads.

    We want to study \(P(H|F_n)\) , let \(C_i\) be the event that the \(i^{th}\) coin is selected.
    $$
    P(E|F)=\sum_{i=0}^kP(E|F_iF)P(F_i|F)\Rightarrow P(H|F_n)=\sum_{i=0}^kP(H|F_nC_i)P(C_i|F_n)
    $$
    \(H,F_n\) are conditional independence given \(C_i\) (common sense) \(P(H|F_nC_i)=P(H|C_i)=\frac{i}{k}\)

    Using the second Bayes's Formula
    $$
    \begin{align}
    P(C_i|F_n)=&\frac{P(F_i|C_i)P(C_i)}{\sum_{j=0}^{l}P(F_n|C_j)P(C_j)}
    \=&\frac{(\frac{i}{k})^n}{\sum_{j=0}^{k}(\frac{j}{k})^n}
    \P(H|F_n)&=\frac{\sum_{i=0}^{k}(\frac{i}{k})^{n+1}}{\sum_{j=0}^{k}(\frac{j}{k})^n}When k is large.
    \\frac{1}{k}\sum_{i=0}^k(\frac{i}{k})^{n+1}&\approx\int_{0}^{1}x^{n+1}dx=\frac{1}{n+2}
    \\frac{1}{k}\sum_{j=0}^k(\frac{i}{k})^{n}&\approx\int_{0}^{1}x^{n}dx=\frac{1}{n+1}
    \P(H|F_n)&\approx\frac{n+1}{n+2}. When k is large.
    \end{align}
    $$
